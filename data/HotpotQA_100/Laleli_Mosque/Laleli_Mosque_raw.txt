Laleli Mosque

The Laleli Mosque (Turkish\ref{Turkish_language}: "Laleli Camii, or Tulip Mosque" ) is an 18th-century Ottoman imperial mosque\ref{Ottoman_imperial_mosque} located in Laleli\ref{Laleli,_Fatih}, Fatih\ref{Fatih}, Istanbul\ref{Istanbul}, Turkey\ref{Turkey}.

The Laleli Mosque was built by Sultan\ref{Sultan} Mustafa III\ref{Mustafa_III} from 1760–1763, designed in the baroque\ref{baroque} style by Ottoman imperial architect Mehmet Tahir Ağa\ref{Mehmet_Tahir_Ağa}.

The complex was destroyed by a fire in 1783 shortly after its completion and was immediately rebuilt. A fire in 1911 destroyed the madrasah\ref{madrasah}, and subsequent road construction work destroyed many other auxiliary structures to the mosque.

The mosque was built on a high terrace over a complex of vaulted shops, whose rents were intended to financially support the mosque complex. Underneath the mosque structure itself is a great hall, supported by eight enormous pillars with a fountain in the center.

The mosque is oriented along a northwest-southeast axis, and has a rectangular courtyard about twice the size of the prayer hall to the northwest. This courtyard has a continuous arcade with eighteen domed bays and an ablution\ref{Wudu} fountain in the center. The mosque itself has a brick and masonry base, with a masonry superstructure and octagonal drum supporting the dome. There are two minaret\ref{minaret}s located at either end of the portico\ref{portico} flanking the courtyard entrances.

The Laleli Mosque is an octagon inscribed within a rectangle, with a gallery on its western end. The walls make use of colorful variegated marble\ref{marble}s in red, blue, yellow and browns, further decorated with medallions in opus sectile\ref{opus_sectile} using also semi-precious onyx\ref{onyx} and jasper\ref{jasper}s. The mihrab\ref{mihrab} and mimbar\ref{mimbar} are likewise richly decorated with precious marbles. The interior is well lit, with numerous windows in combinations of white and stained glass\ref{stained_glass}.

The dome is 12.50 m in diameter and 24.50 m high on an octagonal drum of eight arches, with semi-dome\ref{semi-dome}s at the corner arches and larger semi-domes joining the arches above the mihrab and the central bay of the narthex\ref{narthex}.

Most of the structures of the Laleli Mosque "kulliye\ref{kulliye}" have disappeared over the years, but octagonal domed "türbe\ref{türbe}" facing Ordu Street remains, and contains the graves of Mustafa III\ref{Mustafa_III}, his wife Mihrisah Sultan, son Selim III\ref{Selim_III} and daughters Hibetullah and Fatma Sultan and Mihrimah Sultan. The interior is decorated with İznik tiles\ref{İznik_pottery}, and a band of calligraphy encircles the upper walls.

The Tayyare Apartments\ref{Tayyare_Apartments} were built in 1922 next to the mosque on the ground of 1911 burnt down Koska Madrasa\ref{Madrasa}, which belonged to the mosque's complex. The buildings were redeveloped into a five-star hotel.
