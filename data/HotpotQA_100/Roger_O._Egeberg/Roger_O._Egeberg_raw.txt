Roger O. Egeberg

Roger Olaf Egeberg, M.D. (13 November 1902 – 13 September 1997 Washington, D.C.\ref{Washington,_D.C.}) was an American medical educator, administrator and advocate of public health\ref{public_health}. He was General Douglas MacArthur\ref{General_Douglas_MacArthur}'s personal physician during World War II\ref{World_War_II} in the Pacific theater\ref{Pacific_Ocean_theatre_of_World_War_II}. His other roles included Assistant Secretary for Health and Scientific Affairs in the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (now the United States Department of Health and Human Services\ref{United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services}) during the Nixon\ref{Richard_Nixon} administration and Dean of the University of Southern California\ref{University_of_Southern_California}'s medical school.

Egeberg was born in Chicago, Illinois\ref{Chicago,_Illinois} to Hans Olaf Egeberg (1875-1932) and Ulrikka Rostrup Egeberg (1875-1932) (née Nielsen), a Norwegian immigrant family. Egeberg married a Canadian\ref{Canadians} sculptor, Margaret Chahoon (25 August 1904 - 31 March 2011), in 1929. Together, they had a son and three daughters.

Egeberg received a bachelor's degree in 1925 from Cornell University\ref{Cornell_University} where he was a member of the Quill and Dagger\ref{Quill_and_Dagger} society. He earned his medical degree in 1929 at Northwestern University\ref{Northwestern_University} school of medicine\ref{Feinberg_School_of_Medicine}. He became an internist and joined the faculty of Western Reserve University School of Medicine\ref{Case_Western_Reserve_University_School_of_Medicine}.

During World War II, Egeberg was a member of the 4th General Hospital which was sent to Australia\ref{Australia}. From there he transferred to Milne Bay\ref{Milne_Bay}, Papua New Guinea\ref{Papua_New_Guinea} (PNG) where he organised field stations and was a malaria\ref{malaria} control officer. Through determined attempts to control illnesses such as malaria and sexually transmitted diseases in PNG, Egeberg was noticed by MacArthur who made him his personal physician and aide-de-camp\ref{aide-de-camp}. Egeberg rose to the rank of colonel\ref{Colonel_(United_States)} and was awarded the Bronze Star Medal\ref{Bronze_Star_Medal} and the Legion of Merit\ref{Legion_of_Merit}.

At the end of the war, Egeberg treated the Prime Minister of Japan\ref{Japan}, Hideki Tojo\ref{Hideki_Tojo} who had shot himself. Tojo was later hanged.

After the war, Egeberg was Chief of Medicine at Wadsworth General Hospital, head of County-University of Southern California Medical Center\ref{LAC+USC_Medical_Center} (1959) and Dean, USC School of Medicine\ref{Keck_School_of_Medicine_of_USC} (1964). After working for Nixon, Egeberg took a professorial chair at Georgetown University. In his later career, he advocated for the advanced training of doctors in geriatric medicine.

From 1969 to 1971, Egeberg was the Assistant Secretary for health and scientific affairs in the Department of Health, Education and Welfare. In this role, he was an advocate for expanding public health\ref{public_health} care and argued for the financing of medical programs (through the Office of Health Care Financing). During this time, Egeberg visited the USSR\ref{USSR} and developed a professional relationship with Boris Vasilevich Petrovsky, the Soviet minister of health. Officials of the department, including Finch, expressed dissatisfaction with Egeberg's managerial skills. From 1971 to 1977, Egeberg was the health secretary's special assistant for health policy and special consultant to the president in health affairs. In 1976 and 1977, he was also special assistant for health education.

In 1970, Egeberg introduced an amendment to legislation governing penalties for marijuana\ref{cannabis_(drug)} offences. The nature of a conviction for possession of marijuana was changed from felony to misdemeanor.

The committee reviewing the Controlled Substances Act\ref{Controlled_Substances_Act} requested Egeberg's advice on how marijuana should be regulated. After Egeberg wrote to Harley O. Staggers\ref{Harley_O._Staggers}, chairman of the house committee on interstate and foreign commerce, marijuana was temporarily placed in Schedule I, the most tightly-controlled category of drugs.
