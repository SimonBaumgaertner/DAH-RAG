Back to Mono (1958–1969)

Back to Mono (1958–1969) is a box set\ref{box_set} (4 compact discs or 5 vinyl LPs) compilation of the recorded work of record producer\ref{record_producer} Phil Spector\ref{Phil_Spector}, through the 1960s, released in 1991 by ABKCO\ref{ABKCO_Records} as #7118-2. The first track, "To Know Him Is to Love Him\ref{To_Know_Him_Is_to_Love_Him}," released in 1958, features Spector performing as part of the group the Teddy Bears\ref{The_Teddy_Bears_(band)}. Initially a vinyl album\ref{LP_record}-sized package, the box contained a booklet with photographs, complete song lyrics, discographical\ref{discography} information, and a reproduction of the essay on Spector by Tom Wolfe\ref{Tom_Wolfe}, "The First Tycoon of Teen." The package also contained a small, round, red "Back to Mono" pin.

"Back to Mono" surveys recordings leading up to and featuring the Wall of Sound\ref{Wall_of_Sound}, Spector's famed production trademark. Starting with "He's A Rebel\ref{He's_A_Rebel}" on disc one, through "I'll Never Need More Than This\ref{I'll_Never_Need_More_Than_This}" and including disc four, all of the musical arrangements\ref{arrangements} are by Jack Nitzsche\ref{Jack_Nitzsche}. The list of musicians contributing to the tracks number in the hundreds, but prominent among them are Brian Wilson\ref{Brian_Wilson}, Hal Blaine\ref{Hal_Blaine}, Sonny Bono\ref{Sonny_Bono}, Red Callender\ref{Red_Callender}, Glen Campbell\ref{Glen_Campbell}, Al DeLory\ref{Al_DeLory}, Steve Douglas\ref{Steve_Douglas_(saxophonist)}, Jim Gordon\ref{Jim_Gordon_(musician)}, Jim Horn\ref{Jim_Horn}, Carol Kaye\ref{Carol_Kaye}, Barney Kessel\ref{Barney_Kessel}, Larry Knechtel\ref{Larry_Knechtel}, Jay Migliori\ref{Jay_Migliori}, Ray Pohlman\ref{Ray_Pohlman}, Don Randi\ref{Don_Randi}, Lyle Ritz\ref{Lyle_Ritz}, Billy Strange\ref{Billy_Strange}, Tommy Tedesco\ref{Tommy_Tedesco}, Bill Pitman\ref{Bill_Pitman}, and Nino Tempo\ref{Nino_Tempo}.

The set collects every single\ref{single_(music)} produced by Spector and issued on the Philles label\ref{Philles_Records}, with the exception of Philles 132, "White Cliffs of Dover\ref{(There'll_Be_Bluebirds_Over)_The_White_Cliffs_of_Dover}" by The Righteous Brothers\ref{The_Righteous_Brothers}, which did not chart. Of the catalogue items Philles 100 through 136, the set omits: Philles 101, 103, and 104, produced by Spector's early business partner and label namesake, Lester Sill\ref{Lester_Sill}; Philles 133, produced by Jeff Barry\ref{Jeff_Barry}, although its B-side\ref{B-side} produced by Spector, "When I Saw You," does appear; and Philles 134, produced by Bob Crewe\ref{Bob_Crewe}. Philles 105, withdrawn initially in 1962 owing to its controversial subject matter, has also been included. Both "Walking in the Rain\ref{Walking_in_the_Rain_(The_Ronettes_song)}" by The Ronettes\ref{The_Ronettes} and "Stumble and Fall" by Darlene Love\ref{Darlene_Love} are listed in most discographies, including the box booklet, as Philles 123. The single "Christmas (Baby Please Come Home)\ref{Christmas_(Baby_Please_Come_Home)}," initially released in 1963 as Philles 119, was re-released the following year as Philles 125 with a different B-side, and did not chart either year. Philles 134 through 136 were not released in the United States\ref{United_States}; however, there are DJ promo pressings of 136.

In addition to the Philles singles catalogue, the first three discs present Spector productions for other labels, various unreleased material, and two album\ref{LP_album} tracks from "Presenting the Fabulous Ronettes featuring Veronica\ref{Presenting_the_Fabulous_Ronettes_featuring_Veronica}", Philles 4006, and one from "River Deep - Mountain High\ref{River_Deep_-_Mountain_High_(album)}", Philles 4011. Spector created additional imprints for specific projects, such as the Phil Spector label\ref{Phil_Spector_Records} to promote the solo career of Veronica "Ronnie" Bennett\ref{Ronnie_Spector} of the Ronettes\ref{the_Ronettes}, the future Mrs. Spector. Some of those items also appear on these discs.

The fourth disc contains the entirety of the holiday\ref{Christmas} album released on the same day as the JFK assassination\ref{JFK_assassination}, "A Christmas Gift for You\ref{A_Christmas_Gift_for_You}", purportedly the only long-playing album from his Philles heyday that Spector worked on with the same intensity that he generally lavished on the singles. It peaked at #13 on the Christmas Albums chart in 1963, although a reissue in 1972 went to #6.

In 2003, the album was ranked number 64 on "Rolling Stone\ref{Rolling_Stone}" magazine's list of the 500 greatest albums of all time\ref{The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time}. With the exception of an anthology of his productions for the girl group\ref{girl_group} the Paris Sisters\ref{the_Paris_Sisters}, "Back to Mono" represents the totality of Spector's singles from this period in print, as of Spring, 2007.
