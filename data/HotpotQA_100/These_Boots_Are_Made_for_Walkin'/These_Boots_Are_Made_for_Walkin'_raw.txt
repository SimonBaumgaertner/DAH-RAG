These Boots Are Made for Walkin'

"These Boots Are Made for Walkin' is a hit song written by Lee Hazlewood\ref{Lee_Hazlewood} and recorded by Nancy Sinatra\ref{Nancy_Sinatra}. It charted January 22, 1966\ref{1966-01-22} and reached No. 1 in the United States "Billboard" Hot 100\ref{Billboard_Hot_100} and in the UK Singles Chart\ref{UK_Singles_Chart}.

Subsequently, many cover versions of the song have been released in a range of styles: metal, pop, rock, punk rock, country, dance, and industrial. Loretta Lynn\ref{Loretta_Lynn}, Jessica Simpson\ref{Jessica_Simpson}, Kon Kan\ref{Kon_Kan}, Geri Halliwell\ref{Geri_Halliwell}, The Residents\ref{The_Residents}, Megadeth\ref{Megadeth}, Jewel\ref{Jewel_(singer)}, Operation Ivy\ref{Operation_Ivy_(band)}, Parquet Courts\ref{Parquet_Courts}, and KMFDM\ref{KMFDM} also released covers of the song. Leningrad Cowboys\ref{Leningrad_Cowboys} titled their version "These Boots", and released a video of the song, directed by Aki Kaurismäki\ref{Aki_Kaurismäki}.

Nancy Sinatra was encouraged by Lee Hazlewood to sing the song as if she were "a sixteen-year-old girl who fucks truck drivers". Sinatra's recording of the song was made with the help of Los Angeles\ref{Los_Angeles} session musician\ref{session_musician}s known as the Wrecking Crew\ref{The_Wrecking_Crew_(music)}.

This session included Hal Blaine\ref{Hal_Blaine} on drums, Al Casey\ref{Al_Casey_(rock_&amp;_roll_guitarist)}, Tommy Tedesco\ref{Tommy_Tedesco}, and Billy Strange\ref{Billy_Strange} on guitars, Ollie Mitchell\ref{Ollie_Mitchell}, Roy Caton\ref{Roy_Caton} and Lew McCreary on horns, Carol Kaye\ref{Carol_Kaye} on electric bass and Chuck Berghofer\ref{Chuck_Berghofer} on double bass\ref{double_bass}, providing the notable bass line. Nick Bonney was the guitarist for the Nelson Riddle\ref{Nelson_Riddle} Orchestra.

According to Carol Kaye\ref{Carol_Kaye}, "Arranger Billy Strange believed in using the two basses together. Producer Lee Hazlewood asked Chuck to put a sliding run on the front of the tune. Chuck complied by playing notes about three tones apart (4–6 frets apart), but Lee stopped the take. 'No Chuck, make your sliding notes closer together', and that is what you hear."

According to Al Casey, "Well, Lee and I had been friends forever, and he said, 'I've got this song I'm working on, and I want the guitar to play this.' And he showed me, because there's a little bit more than banging on an 'E-chord', which is what most people do. There's more to it than that. He said, 'I want you to do this on the song,' and he sang the song and played the rhythm guitar lick, and I went 'Oh, that's cute!', little suspecting it was gonna be huge."

Nancy Sinatra\ref{Nancy_Sinatra} would later record one of Don Lanier\ref{Don_Lanier}'s songs on her 1969 album "Nancy\ref{Nancy_(album)}".

The second single taken from her debut album "Boots\ref{Boots_(album)}", and follow-up to the minor hit "So Long, Babe", the song became an instant success. In late February 1966, the song topped the "Billboard" Hot 100\ref{Billboard_Hot_100} chart, a move it replicated in similar charts across the world.

When the single was first released, some thought it had to do with the subway strike in New York\ref{1966_New_York_City_transit_strike}.

In the same year Sinatra recorded a promotional film, which would later be known as the music video\ref{music_video}, for the song. It was produced by Color-Sonics and played on Scopitone\ref{Scopitone} video jukebox\ref{jukebox}es. In 1986, for the song's 20th anniversary, cable station VH1\ref{VH1} played the video.

Sinatra told Alison Martino\ref{Alison_Martino} that other videos and performances are from TV shows like "The Ed Sullivan Show\ref{The_Ed_Sullivan_Show}", "Hullaballoo\ref{Hullabaloo_(TV_series)}" and "Shindig!\ref{Shindig!}"

The videos featured Sinatra wearing an iconic pair of boots.

In 2006, Pitchfork Media\ref{Pitchfork_Media} selected it as the 114th best song of the 1960s. Critic Tom Breihan described the song as "maybe the finest bitchy kiss-off in pop history".

Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company\ref{Goodyear_Tire_and_Rubber_Company} used portions of the song for its 1960s ad campaign promoting its "wide boots" tires. Nancy Sinatra unsuccessfully sued Goodyear for using the song, claiming that it had violated her publicity rights.

The song was included in the third episode of American action-comedy series "The Good Guys\ref{The_Good_Guys_(2010_TV_series)}", "Broken Door Theory".

British singer-songwriter Geri Halliwell\ref{Geri_Halliwell} recorded her own version of "These Boots Are Made for Walkin for the soundtrack to the film "". The song was released on February 1, 2000 to promote previously the film. It was also included as a B-Side in her single Bag It Up\ref{Bag_It_Up}, ranking number one in the UK charts.

Halliwell performed the song in Casa Milà\ref{Casa_Milà} in July 2000, and again in Oman\ref{Oman} in November 2001.

Jessica Simpson\ref{Jessica_Simpson} recorded her own version of "These Boots Are Made for Walkin (and added her own lyrics) for the soundtrack\ref{soundtrack} to the film "The Dukes of Hazzard\ref{The_Dukes_of_Hazzard_(film)}" (2005). The version was also included in the international version of her fifth studio album, "A Public Affair\ref{A_Public_Affair}" (2006). Simpson's cover was co-produced by Jimmy Jam and Terry Lewis\ref{Jimmy_Jam_and_Terry_Lewis} and was released as the soundtrack's first single\ref{Single_(music)} in 2005. It became Simpson's fifth top-twenty single in the United States\ref{United_States}, and its music video\ref{music_video} drew some controversy because of its sexual imagery.

Simpson's version of the song is performed from the point of view of her character in "The Dukes of Hazzard", Daisy Duke\ref{Daisy_Duke}, and it has several major differences from Sinatra's version. The song's lyrics were changed almost completely as Simpson felt that they did not accurately convey the feelings needed for the film; in the original Sinatra dealt with a cheating boyfriend, while in the new version Simpson explored Daisy Duke's personality and experiences. She rewrote the majority of the lyrics herself, although some elements were retained such as the opening line "You keep saying you got something for me..." and the spoken "Are you ready, boots? Start walkin.

Simpson also added some new music to her version of the song. Whereas the original version did not have a bridge\ref{bridge_(music)}, she created one for the cover. A risqué rap-like/spoken breakdown was added after the bridge. Because of the legalities of songwriting\ref{songwriting}, Simpson has not been credited for the new music or lyrics that she wrote. The production of the song was altered as well. Producers Jimmy Jam and Terry Lewis gave the cover a country\ref{country_music}-inspired production because of its relationship to the film "The Dukes of Hazzard", but they also added a more hip hop\ref{hip_hop_music}-like beat.

In an interview with GAC Nights, Jessica stated that her record label did not want to promote the song because of its country feel, even though the song is more pop than country. She said that she told the label "It's a great song and Willie Nelson's on it with me" and she said the label told her pop radio wouldn't understand that importance.

Megadeth\ref{Megadeth} covered the song on their 1985 debut album "Killing Is My Business... and Business Is Good!\ref{Killing_Is_My_Business..._and_Business_Is_Good!}", which is track four on the original release and eight on the 2002 re-release. Their version (entitled "These Boots") featured altered lyrics, and produced more as a parody than a true cover.

When the album started selling well, the writer of the song, Lee Hazlewood\ref{Lee_Hazlewood}, began demanding that the song be omitted, due to its being a "perversion of the original". Megadeth guitarist and frontman Dave Mustaine\ref{Dave_Mustaine} made the point that Hazlewood had been paid royalties\ref{royalties} for years before he made the complaint, although Mustaine eventually omitted the song anyway from newer pressings of the album. When the album was remixed in 2002, a censored version of the song was included as a bonus track. In 2011, an uncensored live version recorded in 1987 was released as part of the 25th anniversary edition of the album "Peace Sells... but Who's Buying?\ref{Peace_Sells..._but_Who's_Buying?}".

In the 1995 documentary film\ref{documentary_film} "", the 74-year-old Dika Newlin\ref{Dika_Newlin}, dressed in leather and backed by the band Apocowlypso, performed a punk rock\ref{punk_rock} version of the song in a concert sequence.

Nicki Gillis\ref{Nicki_Gillis} covered the song on her 2011 album "Woman of Substance". The song has featured in Nicki's live shows across the globe since she was 19 years old. Current performances retain the faithfulness of Nancy's original vocals but with a contemporary heavier rock sound played by Nicki's band. The version on her "Woman of Substance" album is a touch softer and more like the original Nancy Sinatra version from 1966.

Marcia Hines\ref{Marcia_Hines} covered the song in 2015, specifically for the trailer of season two of "The Real Housewives of Melbourne\ref{The_Real_Housewives_of_Melbourne}".
