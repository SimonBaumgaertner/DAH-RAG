Adriana Trigiani

Adriana Trigiani is an Italian American\ref{Italian_American} best-selling author\ref{best-selling_author} of sixteen books, television writer\ref{television_writer}, film director\ref{film_director}, and entrepreneur based in Greenwich Village\ref{Greenwich_Village}, New York City\ref{New_York_City}. Trigiani has published a novel a year since 2000.

Inspired by her Italian American\ref{Italian_American} heritage and Appalachian childhood in Big Stone Gap, Virginia\ref{Big_Stone_Gap,_Virginia}, Trigiani arrived in New York in 1985 after attending Saint Mary's College\ref{Saint_Mary's_College_(Indiana)} in Indiana\ref{Indiana}. Trigiani made her off-Broadway debut in New York City as a playwright in 1985 at the Manhattan Theater Club\ref{Manhattan_Theater_Club} with "Secrets of the Lava Lamp," directed by Stuart Ross. From 1988-98, she created scripts for television sitcoms, including "The Cosby Show\ref{The_Cosby_Show}" (1984) and its spin-off "A Different World\ref{A_Different_World}" (1987). She was the writer and executive producer of "City Kids" for ABC\ref{Capital_Cities_Communications}/Jim Henson\ref{Jim_Henson} Productions, and she oversaw "Growing Up Funny," a television special for Lifetime\ref{Lifetime_(TV_network)}, and "Linc's," a Showtime\ref{Showtime_(TV_network)} comedy series. Trigiani has written best-selling novels, screenplays, and two memoirs, and she has written and directed documentaries and a major motion picture. Trigiani co-founded "The Origin Project," a writing education program for school children.

Trigiani authored the best-selling "Big Stone Gap" series, including "Big Stone Gap" (2000), "Big Cherry Holler" (2001), "Milk Glass Moon" (2002), and "Home to Big Stone Gap" (2006), set in her Virginia\ref{Virginia} hometown; and the bestselling "Valentine trilogy," the tale of a woman working to save her family's shoe company in Greenwich Village\ref{Greenwich_Village}. Trigiani also wrote the "Viola" books, about a clever teenage filmmaker from Brooklyn\ref{Brooklyn}, for young adults. Trigiani's acclaimed stand-alone novels include "Lucia, Lucia" (2003), "The Queen of the Big Time" (2004), and "Rococo" (2005). Trigiani's book "The Shoemaker's Wife" is the fictional account of the lives of her own grandparents after emigrating to America from Italy\ref{Italy} in the early 20th century. Regularly on the New York Times\ref{New_York_Times} Bestsellers list, critics have noted Trigiani's ability to "create distinctive voices for each of her characters." Millions of copies of Trigiani's books are in print in the United States and published in 36 countries around the world. Overlapping themes include self-perception, social identity, the universal immigrant story, personal loss, working class life, and contemporary social and environmental issues. Since 2012, "Adriana Trigiani Tours," and "AT Escapes," have offered travel tours to Italy\ref{Italy}, Scotland\ref{Scotland}, Spain\ref{Spain} and Gibraltar\ref{Gibraltar} inspired by the novels of Adriana Trigiani.

During the 1990s, Trigiani wrote and directed an award winning documentary "Queen of The Big Time" (1996), the story of her father’s hometown of Roseto\ref{Roseto,_Pennsylvania}, Pennsylvania\ref{Pennsylvania}, shown in film festivals in London\ref{London} and Hong Kong\ref{Hong_Kong}, co-produced "Green Chimneys," and later contributed to PBS\ref{PBS} documentary "The Italian Americans\ref{Italian_Americans}." In 2014, Trigiani directed the major motion picture "Big Stone Gap (film)\ref{Big_Stone_Gap_(film)}," a romantic comedy film\ref{romantic_comedy_film} adaptation of her namesake bestselling novel, produced by Donna Gigliotti\ref{Donna_Gigliotti} for Altar Identity Studios, a subsidiary of Media Society. "Big Stone Gap (film)\ref{Big_Stone_Gap_(film)}" is a story of family secrets and self-discovery in an Appalachian coal-mining town of the late 1970s. The award-winning ensemble cast includes Ashley Judd\ref{Ashley_Judd}, Whoopi Goldberg\ref{Whoopi_Goldberg}, Jane Krakowski\ref{Jane_Krakowski}, Jenna Elfman\ref{Jenna_Elfman}, and Patrick Wilson\ref{Patrick_Wilson_(American_actor)}. Released on October 9, 2015 by Picturehouse (company)\ref{Picturehouse_(company)}, Trigiani's narrative directorial debut arrived nearly 30-year after the sale of her first screenplay, "Three to Get Married," produced by Kate Benton in 1986. Opening the Virginia Film Festival\ref{Virginia_Film_Festival}, "Big Stone Gap (film)\ref{Big_Stone_Gap_(film)}" was ranked among the top 250 grossing women directed films of 2014.

Trigiani and her work have regularly been featured on NBC\ref{NBC}'s "Today Show\ref{Today_(U.S._TV_program)}," "CBS\ref{CBS} This Morning," WNYC\ref{WNYC}’s The Leonard Lopate Show\ref{Leonard_Lopate_Show}, and the "Diane Rehm\ref{Diane_Rehm} Show" on "National Public Radio\ref{National_Public_Radio}. Trigiani's community service works include guest speaker at New York University\ref{New_York_University} and the New School for Social Research\ref{New_School_for_Social_Research}, commencement speaker at University of Virginia\ref{University_of_Virginia} at Wise, and University of New Haven\ref{University_of_New_Haven}, Connecticut, and for 10 years, host of the "Library of Virginia Literary Awards," and also the "Poets and Writers Gala" of New York City\ref{New_York_City}, The Audio Publishers Association "Audies Gala," the Barnes & Noble\ref{Barnes_&amp;_Noble} "Writers for Writers Awards," and co-host of the Arizona\ref{Arizona} "Women's Board Annual Authors Luncheon."
