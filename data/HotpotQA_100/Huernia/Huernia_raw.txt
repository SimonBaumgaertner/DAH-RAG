Huernia

The genus Huernia (family Apocynaceae\ref{Apocynaceae}, subfamily Asclepiadoideae\ref{Asclepiadoideae}) consists of stem succulent\ref{stem_succulent}s from Eastern and Southern Africa\ref{Africa}, first described as a genus in 1810. The flower\ref{flower}s are five-lobed, usually somewhat more funnel- or bell-shaped than in the closely related genus\ref{genus} "Stapelia\ref{Stapelia}", and often striped vividly in contrasting colours or tones, some glossy, others matt and wrinkled depending on the species concerned. To pollinate\ref{pollinate}, the flower\ref{flower}s attract flies by emitting a scent similar to that of carrion\ref{carrion}. The genus is considered close to the genera "Stapelia\ref{Stapelia}" and "Hoodia\ref{Hoodia}". The name is in honour of Justin Heurnius (1587–1652) a Dutch\ref{Dutch_people} missionary\ref{missionary} who is reputed to have been the first collector of South African\ref{South_African} Cape\ref{Cape_Floristic_Region} plants. His name was actually mis-spelt by the collector.

Various species of "Huernia" are considered famine food\ref{famine_food} by the inhabitants of Konso special woreda\ref{Konso_special_woreda} in southern Ethiopia\ref{Ethiopia}. The local inhabitants, who call the native species of this genus "baqibaqa" indiscriminately, eat it with prepared balls of sorghum\ref{sorghum}; they note that "baqibaqa" tastes relatively good and has no unpleasant side-effects when boiled and consumed. As a result, local farmers encouraged it to grow on stone walls forming the terraces, where it does not compete with other crops.

Phylogenetic studies have shown the genus to be monophyletic, and most closely related to the "Tavaresia\ref{Tavaresia}" genus, and to a widespread branch of stapeliads comprising the genera "Orbea\ref{Orbea_(plant)}", "Piaranthus\ref{Piaranthus}" and "Stapelia\ref{Stapelia}".
